Does Tamil Nadu’s Startup Ecosystem Recurse?
A Research Report on Ecosystem Formation, with Case Studies
Companion analysis to the essay series. Prepared from public records, official StartupTN
communications, primary program documents, and named-source reporting. Every loadbearing claim is tagged Confirmed or Emerging.

Executive Summary
Tamil Nadu is provisioning startup-ecosystem components on a large scale and has
deliberately engineered connection points into nearly every program it runs. The decisive
question is not whether components exist — they plainly do — but whether they connect
into self-reinforcing loops that would keep turning if the state stepped back.
On the evidence, the answer is layered and specific:
1. Provisioning is comprehensive. The state funds talent, early capital, market access,
deep-tech anchoring, and convening.
2. Connection is intentional. Accelerators bolted onto grants, “Smart Cards” bridging
hackathons to hubs, equity stakes that keep the state on cap tables, and collegeindustry networks are all explicit attempts to wire components together.
3. Some loops are demonstrably closing. TANSEED is the clearest state-built loop, with
roughly ₹40 crore of grants associated with about ₹535 crore in downstream private
funding and several verifiable founder progressions. The strongest loops overall — the
Zoho/Freshworks alumni network and IIT Madras Research Park — are organic anchors
the state inherited rather than created.
4. Other loops remain open. Public procurement, the SC/ST fund’s leadership pipeline,
the women-founder network, and the hackathon-to-company pathway are all
connection-attempted but closure-unproven.
5. The crucial gap is specific. Almost every confirmed case demonstrates a grant →
validation → scale arc. What remains largely undocumented for the state’s newer
programs is the final turn of recursion — successful founders recirculating as mentors
and investors for the next cohort. That turn is what makes the Zoho network a true loop,
and the state can enable it but cannot provision it.

Bottom line: Ecosystem formation in Tamil Nadu is real and accelerating; its self-sustaining
closure is still partly underwritten by the state. The defining question for the next five years
is whether the state’s successful founders will fund and mentor the next ones without being
asked.

1. Framework: Components, Connections, and Closure
A startup ecosystem is frequently described as a collection of inputs — talent, capital,
mentors, infrastructure, customers. But inputs alone do not constitute an ecosystem any
more than bricks constitute a building. What distinguishes an ecosystem is recursion:
outputs that re-enter as inputs. Founders generate employees who become founders; exits
generate capital that funds new ventures; reputations attract talent that produces new
reputations. When these feedback paths are strong enough, the system becomes selfsustaining.
This report uses three precise terms:
Component — a provisioned input (e.g., a seed grant, a hackathon, a research park).
Connection — a deliberate mechanism linking one component to another (e.g., an
accelerator that routes grant recipients to investors).
Loop closure — evidence that the connection actually produces a feedback effect (e.g.,
a funded founder who later mentors or finances the next cohort).
The canonical closed loop is the “founder flywheel”: founder → employees → new
founders → investors → new founders. This report treats that flywheel as the benchmark
and asks, for each Tamil Nadu initiative, which turns of the loop are evidenced.

2. Methodology and Evidence Standards
Claims in this report are graded:
Confirmed — supported by a primary source (an official StartupTN post, a program
document, a regulatory filing) or by multiple named secondary sources.
Emerging — the component and the connection are documented, but loop closure is
not yet evidenced in public records.
Two methodological cautions were applied throughout, both learned the hard way during
research:

1. Verify the connection, not just the entity. A real company and a real funding event do
not establish a real link between them and a program. Each causal claim (e.g., “X scaled
because of TANSEED”) was checked against a source that asserts the link directly.
2. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. Several claims that could not
initially be found through general search proved correct once primary sources (official
posts, the full text of cited reports) were consulted. Where a claim could not be verified,
it is marked Emerging rather than dismissed.
Limitations. Much program-performance data is self-reported by StartupTN or
beneficiaries; aggregate figures (e.g., follow-on funding) are not independently audited
here. Several programs are recent (2024–2026), so the absence of closure evidence often
reflects program youth rather than failure. Figures are current to mid-2026.

3. The Provisioning Layer
Tamil Nadu, primarily through StartupTN (the Tamil Nadu Startup and Innovation Mission,
est. 2018), has built an instrument for nearly every layer of the startup stack.
Talent: Naan Mudhalvan, the state skilling mission, and its flagship Niral Thiruvizha
hackathon, which in a recent edition drew on the order of 28,000 students across
roughly 290 institutions.
Early capital: TANSEED seed grants (₹10–15 lakh), a dedicated SC/ST Startup Fund, a
women-led grant track, plus angel-network and investor-connect programs and
thematic funds (including a Space Tech fund).
Market access: a procurement framework that relaxes earnest-money, turnover, and
prior-experience requirements and permits direct purchases from startups.
Deep-tech anchoring: IIT Madras Research Park and the iTNT Hub, linking startups to
laboratories, faculty, and industry.
Convening: the annual Umagine TN summit.
The provisioning question is therefore settled. The components exist, and they cover the
stack. The analytical action is entirely in the connection and closure layers.

4. The Connection Layer
What distinguishes Tamil Nadu’s recent design is that it does not merely fund components;
it tries to wire them together.

TANSEED evolved from a one-time cheque into a relationship: from its fifth edition it
takes a ~3% support stake and enrolls recipients in a year-long accelerator with
mentorship, national and international delegations, and post-investment reviews.
Niral Thiruvizha issues finalists StartupTN “Smart Cards” that route them into regional
hubs and incubators.
The SC/ST Fund invests via equity and compulsorily convertible debentures,
deliberately making the government a shareholder rather than a donor, with postinvestment support attached.
iTNT Hub is structured explicitly as a connector across 570+ engineering colleges,
researchers, and industry, and operates a Technology Transfer Facilitation Centre.
TN-RISE is built as a women founders’ council and co-creation centre with mentorship
MoUs.
These are connection mechanisms, not just funding lines. The state has grasped that an
ecosystem lives in the edges between nodes, not only the nodes.

5. Case Studies
Each case study follows the same frame: Context · What was provisioned and connected
· Evidence of recursion · Loop status (which turns are evidenced) · Verification ·
Assessment.

Case Study 1 — The Zoho / Freshworks Founder Flywheel (the benchmark)
Context. Zoho (and its spin-out Freshworks) turned Chennai into one of India’s principal
SaaS hubs over two decades, with no decisive role from state programs.
Provisioned / connected. Not by the state. The “connection” here is cultural and organic: a
large, skills-first employer that trained engineers who later founded their own companies.
Evidence of recursion. On the order of 59 former Zoho employees became startup
founders, seeding companies such as Freshworks, Chargebee, and Facilio, and creating a
dense SaaS cluster in Chennai. Crucially, the loop runs all the way around: alumni founders
have become angel investors and mentors to the next generation.
Loop status. Fully closed. This is the rare case where every turn of the flywheel — founder
→ employee → founder → investor → new founder — is evidenced.
Verification. Confirmed (Inc42; Vertex Ventures analysis).

Assessment. This is the proof that recursion is achievable in Tamil Nadu — and the
reference against which state programs should be measured. It is also a caution: it shows
that the hardest turn of the loop (founders becoming financiers of the next cohort) emerged
organically over decades, not through a policy instrument.

Case Study 2 — IIT Madras Research Park / IITMIC (the institutional anchor)
Context. India’s first university research park, paired with the IIT Madras Incubation Cell,
has become the country’s leading deep-tech incubation engine.
Provisioned / connected. Lab access, faculty linkage, IP support, an investor network, and
— importantly — an incubation model that retains a stake in its companies by design so that
founders remain tied to the ecosystem.
Evidence of recursion. 457 startups incubated over roughly twelve years, an unusually
high survival rate (~80% versus the typical 8–10%), about 60% of founders from outside
the IIT system, two unicorns, and an IPO (Ather). Faculty and alumni cycle back as founders
and mentors.
Loop status. Closed, anchored institutionally rather than by individual alumni. The
deliberate cap-table retention is itself a designed recursion mechanism — the incubator has
a structural reason to keep pulling founders back in.
Verification. Confirmed (IIT Madras press release, 2025; YourStory, 2025).
Assessment. A model for how an institution (rather than a market) can manufacture
recursion. The state did not build it, but it is a component the broader ecosystem genuinely
contains.

Case Study 3 — TANSEED (the state’s flagship loop)
Context. TANSEED (Tamil Nadu Startup Seed Fund), launched 2021, is StartupTN’s flagship
early-capital instrument and the clearest test of whether the state can build a loop.
Provisioned / connected. ₹10–15 lakh grants; a ~3% support stake from edition five; a
year-long accelerator with mentorship and delegations; routing into incubators and investor
networks. Since 2021 it has supported roughly 169 startups.
Evidence of recursion (aggregate). Against approximately ₹40 crore allocated, TANSEEDsupported startups have collectively raised on the order of ₹535 crore in external follow-on
funding (StartupTN figures, reported September 2025) — a multiplier that indicates the
grant → private-capital path is real. Named beneficiaries include Buyofuel, Ippopay,
Gallabox, Dream Aerospace, Torus Robotics, Plugzmart, and Greenpod.
Sub-cases (the heart of the evidence):

ippopay (Mohan K, Chennai; fintech/payments). A 2021 TANSEED winner (incubated
at AIC RAISE). The founder publicly describes TANSEED as the company’s “first
recognition,” after which it scaled its payments business. Loop turns evidenced: grant →
validation → scale. Confirmed.
PrimeInvestor (Srikanth Meenakshi, Vidya Bala, Bhavana Acharya; Chennai;
wealthtech/research). A TANSEED 3.0 cohort grantee in 2022 (₹10 lakh),
independently listed among that cohort by the implementing partner. In 2026 it raised
₹19.5 crore from Rainmatter (Zerodha’s investment arm) — its first external funding
round, a description consistent with an earlier non-dilutive state grant. StartupTN
amplified the milestone, explicitly identifying the firm as a TANSEED beneficiary. Loop
turns evidenced: grant → multi-year growth → institutional capital → ecosystem
amplification. Confirmed (StartupTN post; Headstart cohort-3 listing; 2026 funding
coverage).
REUDE Technologies (Reginald Chellam; Tiruvallur/Chennai; drone/UAV). A
TANSEED 4.0 grant recipient and the sole Tamil Nadu company selected among the top
ten finalists in Stanford’s Seed Spark Pitch Demo Day (South Asia cohort). Its flagship
platform serves precision agriculture and defense surveillance. Loop turns evidenced:
grant → international validation. Confirmed (StartupTN post, Aug 2024; corroborated by
Stanford Seed Spark cohort listing and REUDE’s own channels).
HARVTECH (Shrinivasan S, Mohamed Imran; agritech / EV-tractors). Selected from
1,000+ applications for a TANSEED 4.0 grant, stacked with recognition from IIT Madras’s
Carbon Zero Challenge and a BIRAC BIG grant — a portfolio of credentials that
compounds credibility. Loop turns evidenced: grant → stacked validation. Confirmed
(HARVTECH post; YourStory).
Loop status. Partially closed. The grant → validation → scale → follow-on capital arc is
strongly evidenced. What is not yet visible in these cases is the final turn — these founders
becoming the investors or mentors of the next TANSEED cohort.
Verification. Confirmed across aggregate and sub-cases.
Assessment. The most convincing thing the state has built. TANSEED demonstrates that a
small public grant can function as a credibility primer that unlocks private capital. The
recursion is real but currently “half-turn”: money and validation flow forward to founders;
founders have not yet visibly flowed back as capital to successors.

Case Study 4 — Public Procurement: Tamil Nadu vs. Rajasthan (a
comparative / negative case)
Context. “Government as first customer” is a textbook recursion mechanism: a public work
order confers revenue, credibility, and investor confidence, which unlocks private

customers. This case study is instructive precisely because Tamil Nadu has the policy but
not the outcomes, and a peer state shows what closure looks like.
Provisioned / connected. Tamil Nadu relaxes earnest-money, turnover, and priorexperience thresholds and permits direct procurement from startups up to ₹50 lakh via
TANSIM.
Evidence of recursion. Thin. A 2025 Primus Partners report (Table 6) records Tamil Nadu
at roughly five startups awarded work orders worth about ₹0.54 crore — comparable to
Karnataka (₹0.49 crore) and Telangana (~₹1 crore), but far below the leaders.
Contrast — Rajasthan (the closure benchmark). Through its iStart initiative and e-Bazaar
platform (and a landmark amendment to its procurement act), Rajasthan issued 132 work
orders worth over ₹25 crore to 476 startups. The same report’s survey of recipients
found that a large majority reported improved market access and credibility, and around
70% reported increased investor interest after winning government contracts — the
credibility-to-capital loop, operating at scale. Kerala (≈141 startups; ₹17–28 crore across
188 procurements) is a second working model.
Loop status. Open in Tamil Nadu. The connection (policy) exists; the loop (realized orders
→ credibility → private scaling) is barely traced. Closed in Rajasthan, which provides the
proof-of-concept.
Verification. Confirmed (Primus Partners & JITO report, 2025, Table 6).
Assessment. The most actionable gap in this report. Tamil Nadu’s procurement loop is a
policy without a platform. Rajasthan’s e-Bazaar shows that the missing ingredient is not
intent but a transactional engine that converts eligibility into actual orders.

Case Study 5 — SC/ST Startup Fund (the inclusion loop, emerging)
Context. An exclusive equity fund for Scheduled Caste and Scheduled Tribe founders,
designed to address financial exclusion and create role models.
Provisioned / connected. Roughly ₹60.8 crore sanctioned across approximately 26+
startups (₹7.5 crore to five startups in the first batch; ₹9.75 crore to eight in the second;
₹34.6 crore to 26 by end-2023). Investment is via equity and compulsorily convertible
debentures, making the state a shareholder, with post-investment mentorship.
Evidence of recursion. Named, funded companies exist — Ecopmin Technologies
(electronics, ₹3 crore), Adhiban Farms (agritech, ₹2.2 crore), COEO Wellness (₹60 lakh),
Mannvasanai Traditional Food (₹45 lakh), Nanjil Nadu Kani Tribal Women Creations (₹25
lakh), and tribal ventures such as TAMS Tribal Green Fuel. The state-as-shareholder
structure adds credibility for new clients and markets.

Loop status. Emerging. Capital and credibility flow to founders; the program’s stated aim
of producing “inspirational stories” that draw the next cohort, and of alumni moving into
mentorship/leadership, is not yet documented in public records.
Verification. Confirmed funding activity (Business Standard / TICE, 2023; StartupTN). Loop
closure unproven.
Assessment. A well-designed capital-and-credibility instrument whose recursion will
depend on whether early beneficiaries become visible mentors and role models — a
measurable outcome the program should track and publish.

Case Study 6 — Women-Led TANSEED and TN-RISE (the women-founder
network, emerging)
Context. A women-led grant track within TANSEED plus TN-RISE, a women founders’
council launched in July 2024 (World Bank-supported, via the Tamil Nadu Women
Development Corporation / rural transformation project).
Provisioned / connected. ₹15 lakh women-led TANSEED awards; TN-RISE as a co-creation
centre with mentorship MoUs (including Flipkart, HP, and FLO) and a stated target of
mentoring 1,000+ women per year.
Evidence of recursion. At the Global Women Summit 2026 (TNWeSafe), ₹15 lakh TANSEED
awards went to women-led ventures including Dukil Medical and Technical Textiles
(Santhiya Selvakumar, Erode), Aruvi Eco (Kalaiselvi Balamurugan, Tiruppur), Jananom
Natural Nutrition (Coimbatore), Nature Hunt Innovations (Chennai), Shopiqo Innovations
(Tiruvannamalai), and KnappIT Innovation India (Chennai).
Loop status. Emerging. The network and mentorship architecture exist; founder-tofounder peer financing or mentorship closure is not yet documented, and TN-RISE is barely
a year old.
Verification. Confirmed awards (StartupTN post, Global Women Summit 2026). Loop
closure unproven.
Assessment. Strong inclusion infrastructure; closure depends on the council maturing into
an active peer-capital and peer-mentorship network rather than an events calendar.

Case Study 7 — Niral Thiruvizha (the talent funnel, pipeline documented)
Context. A mass student-innovation hackathon under Naan Mudhalvan, intended to convert
student ideas into startups.
Provisioned / connected. Roughly 28,450 students from ~290 institutions in a recent
edition; ₹10,000 prototype grants for the top 1,000 teams, ₹2 lakh innovation vouchers for

the top 50, and StartupTN Smart Cards routing finalists into regional hubs and incubators.
Evidence of recursion. The pipeline is documented end-to-end: for example, KCG College
of Technology had two teams selected among the top fifty for ₹1 lakh grants (144 teams
shortlisted from 837 participants), explicitly “connected with StartupTN regional hubs for
further incubation.”
Loop status. Promising / open. The funnel and the hand-off to incubation are real; a
publicly visible “hackathon team became a funded company” exit is not yet on record.
Verification. Confirmed pipeline (KCG College post, Sept 2024; program documents). Exit
unproven.
Assessment. An excellent top-of-funnel; its recursion claim rests on conversion data the
program does not yet publish.

6. Cross-Case Analysis
Reading the cases together yields four patterns.
(a) State-built loops vs. inherited loops. The two fully closed loops (Zoho network,
IITMRP) are organic or institutional and were not created by StartupTN. The strongest statebuilt loop (TANSEED) is partially closed. The newest state programs (SC/ST leadership,
women networks, hackathon-to-company, procurement) are open. The further a loop is
from the state’s direct control, the more completely it has closed — a sign that the hardest
turns of recursion are market behaviors the state can prompt but not mandate.
(b) The half-turn problem. Nearly every confirmed state case demonstrates the same arc
— grant → validation → scale → follow-on capital — and stops there. The missing turn is
founder → investor/mentor → next founder. This is not a minor omission: it is precisely
the turn that converts a list of funded alumni into a self-sustaining flywheel. Until TANSEED
graduates are visibly funding or mentoring the next cohort, the loop depends on the state to
keep priming it.
(c) Policy without a platform. Procurement is the clearest instance of a connection that
exists on paper but lacks a transactional engine. Rajasthan’s contrast shows the decisive
variable is operational infrastructure (e-Bazaar) plus legal reform, not policy intent.
(d) Program youth is a real confound. TN-RISE (2024), the Deep Tech Policy (2025–26),
and recent TANSEED and women-led cohorts are too young to have produced closure
evidence. “Emerging” here often means “not yet,” not “not working.”
What predicts closure? Across the cases, loops closed where (i) the connector had a

structural reason to keep founders engaged (IITMRP’s cap-table retention; the Zoho
culture), (ii) there was a transactional platform converting eligibility into transactions
(Rajasthan’s e-Bazaar), and (iii) enough time had passed for a first generation of successes
to mature into financiers. Tamil Nadu’s programs have (i) partially (TANSEED’s stake), lack
(ii) in procurement, and are still accumulating (iii) almost everywhere.

8. Findings and Conclusion
Tamil Nadu has done something genuinely sophisticated: it has understood that ecosystems
are recursive and has engineered connection points into almost every component it funds.
In TANSEED it can now point to a real, evidenced loop in which modest public grants are
followed by substantial private capital and international validation. These are not
announcements; they are documented progressions.
But recursion has a final turn the state cannot provision and can only enable — founders
must become the ecosystem’s financiers and mentors for those who follow. The government
can build every edge leading up to that turn; it cannot make the turn itself. Outside the
Zoho/Freshworks network and IIT Madras’s research park, both inherited rather than
created, that traversal is still young.
The verdict is therefore neither “an ecosystem has formed” nor “it is all policy.” It is more
precise: Tamil Nadu’s ecosystem formation is real and accelerating; the components

are in place, the connections are deliberate, and the first state-built loop is closing —
but the system’s self-sustaining closure is still partly underwritten by the state.

9. Recommendations and Indicators to Watch
Recommendations
1. Close the half-turn deliberately. Create explicit pathways and incentives for
TANSEED/SC/ST/women-led alumni to become mentors and angel investors for later
cohorts (e.g., alumni-as-mentor mandates, matched co-investment vehicles for
successful graduates). This is the single highest-leverage intervention.
2. Give procurement a platform, not just a policy. Replicate Rajasthan’s e-Bazaar model
— a transactional catalog with direct work orders and legal reform that converts startup
eligibility into actual orders, with published outcomes.
3. Publish conversion and recirculation data. Niral Thiruvizha should track how many
funnel teams become registered, funded companies; the SC/ST and women programs
should track how many beneficiaries become mentors/investors. Closure that is not
measured cannot be demonstrated.
4. Protect the institutional model. Treat IITMRP’s cap-table-retention design as a
template for keeping founders structurally tied to the ecosystem.
Falsifiable indicators of genuine closure (next 3–5 years)
Named cases of TANSEED/SC/ST/women-led graduates investing in or formally
mentoring later cohorts.
Tamil Nadu’s realized procurement work-order value rising from ~₹0.54 crore toward
peer-state levels.
At least one publicly documented Niral Thiruvizha team reaching funded-company
status.
TN-RISE evolving from an events-and-MoU body into an active peer-capital network
with documented founder-to-founder financing.
If these indicators move, the loops are closing and the ecosystem is becoming selfsustaining. If they do not, Tamil Nadu will remain an impressively provisioned, deliberately
connected ecosystem whose recursion is still state-dependent.
