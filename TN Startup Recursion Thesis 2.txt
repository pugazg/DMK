Provisioning, Connection, and the Unfinished Loop
A Thesis Report on Recursive Ecosystem Formation in Tamil Nadu’s StateLed Startup Programs
Companion analysis to the essay series · Evidence current to mid-2026 · All load-bearing claims graded
Confirmed or Emerging, with causal claims separately flagged, and referenced with source links.

Abstract
A startup ecosystem is not a stockpile of inputs but a set of self-reinforcing feedback loops in which
outputs re-enter as inputs — founders producing employees who become founders, exits producing capital
that funds new ventures. This thesis asks whether Tamil Nadu’s state-led startup programmes are
producing such recursion, or merely provisioning and attempting to connect components. Using a
Component–Connection–Closure framework and a primary-source verification protocol, it examines nine
initiatives through seven structured case studies.
It finds that provisioning is comprehensive, connection is deliberately engineered, and recursion is
genuinely evidenced in several places. But two findings qualify any celebratory reading. First, on
causation: the two fully closed loops (the Zoho/Freshworks alumni network and IIT Madras Research Park)
were inherited rather than state-created, and the flagship instrument, TANSEED, cannot be shown to cause
the outcomes it is associated with. Because TANSEED selects already-promising startups, its association
with roughly ₹535 crore of downstream private capital is as consistent with skilled selection as with
catalysis — and the celebrated cases are precisely those where selection dominates. The state’s evidenced
role is therefore credentialer, convener, and selector, not originator. Second, on distribution: every
measured loop closes through follow-on funding, which dilutes founders and channels equity to external
investors. Recursion and ownership-broadening are distinct axes, and the one fully closed loop is narrow
and elite (urban, English-educated SaaS), while the instruments designed to broaden ownership — the
SC/ST fund, the women-led track, rural and tier-2 programmes — are exactly the loops that have not
closed.
The thesis concludes that ecosystem formation in Tamil Nadu is real and accelerating, but it is neither
clearly state-caused nor, as yet, ownership-widening. Recursion is necessary but not sufficient; closure
among the already-advantaged is not the same as broad-based ownership.

Chapter 1 — Introduction
1.1 Background
Over the past decade Tamil Nadu has moved from a predominantly services-and-manufacturing economy
toward an explicit innovation agenda, institutionalised through the Tamil Nadu Startup and Innovation

Mission (StartupTN/TANSIM, established 2018) and successive startup policies [31], [30]. The state has
registered thousands of startups, issued tens of thousands of “Smart Cards,” and stood up a portfolio of
capital, talent, market-access, and deep-tech instruments [7].

1.2 Problem Statement
Policy evaluations of startup ecosystems frequently count components — grants disbursed, startups
registered, events held — and infer health from their volume. But components are necessary, not sufficient.
The entrepreneurial-ecosystem literature treats ecosystems as systems defined by feedback, not inventory
[1]. This thesis addresses two gaps that volume metrics conceal: the gap between provisioning activity and
recursive self-sustenance, and — once recursion is found — the gap between correlation and causation,
and between ecosystem formation and ownership distribution.

1.3 Research Questions
RQ1. Which components has Tamil Nadu provisioned, and across how much of the startup stack?
RQ2. What deliberate mechanisms connect these components?
RQ3. For which initiatives is recursion (loop closure) evidenced, and for which is it only attempted?
RQ4. What distinguishes loops that have closed from those that have not?
RQ5. Is Tamil Nadu’s startup ecosystem becoming self-sustaining, or does it remain state-dependent?
RQ6. Where recursion is evidenced, is there evidence the state caused it — or selected startups that
would likely have succeeded regardless?
RQ7. Does loop closure broaden ownership, or can a recursing ecosystem concentrate it?

1.4 Objectives
To classify initiatives along the provisioning–connection–closure chain; to verify load-bearing claims
against primary sources while distinguishing relationship from causation; to identify the structural
conditions associated with closure; to test whether closure broadens or concentrates ownership; and to
propose falsifiable indicators by which future self-sustenance can be judged.

1.5 Scope and Significance
The study covers nine StartupTN-adjacent initiatives and two organic/institutional anchors, using public
records to mid-2026. It is significant for its transferable method (Component–Connection–Closure, with
explicit causal and distributional tests) and for framing its conclusions as testable predictions rather than
advocacy.

Chapter 2 — Conceptual Framework and Literature
2.1 Ecosystems as recursive systems
An entrepreneurial ecosystem becomes self-sustaining when outputs feed back as inputs. The strongest
form is the founder flywheel: a successful company trains employees, some found new companies, some

succeed and then invest in or mentor the next generation. Indian venture literature documents this in the
“mafia” pattern — clusters spun out of a single parent, with founders recirculating as angels and mentors
[1], [2].

2.2 The Component–Connection–Closure framework
Three terms operationalise recursion:
Component — a provisioned input (a grant, a hackathon, a research park).
Connection — a deliberate mechanism linking components.
Closure — evidence that a connection produces a feedback effect (a funded founder who later mentors
or finances successors).
The benchmark closed loop is: founder → employees → new founders → investors → new founders. For
each initiative the analysis asks which turns of this loop are evidenced. A loop that demonstrates “grant →
scale” but not “founder → investor → next founder” is partially closed.

2.3 Two axes the framework must keep separate
Recursion is necessary but not sufficient for a healthy ecosystem, and it is a different axis from
ownership distribution. A loop can close vigorously while concentrating ownership, because the usual
closure mechanism — follow-on funding — dilutes founders and transfers equity to external investors and
eventual acquirers. This thesis therefore treats two questions as distinct:
Recursion (formation): do outputs re-enter as inputs?
Ownership (distribution): who ends up holding the equity and economic control the ecosystem
creates — founders and local/community stakeholders, or external capital?
“Ownership-broadening” here means widening the base of people and places that hold durable equity and
control, not merely increasing the number of ventures or the volume of capital cycling through them.
Conflating the two — treating a closing loop as evidence of widening ownership — is the central analytical
hazard this thesis guards against.

2.4 The causal-inference problem
Programme funders select. A seed fund that backs already-promising ventures will show strong
downstream outcomes whether or not it caused them — the classic selection-versus-treatment problem.
Establishing causation requires a counterfactual (a comparison group, a discontinuity around the eligibility
threshold, or at minimum a credible mechanism). Co-occurrence (“X received a grant and later raised
capital”), even when asserted by the funder, is not a counterfactual. This thesis grades relationship and
causation separately.

2.5 Comparators
Internally, the Zoho/Freshworks network and IIT Madras Research Park prove recursion is achievable in
Tamil Nadu [2], [4]. Externally, Rajasthan’s iStart procurement model is a benchmark for a state-built
market-access loop that has closed at scale [24].

Chapter 3 — Methodology
3.1 Research design
A qualitative, multiple-case design. Initiatives are the units of analysis; each is examined on a fixed frame
(context, provisioning, connection, recursion evidence, loop status, verification, assessment) to enable
cross-case comparison.

3.2 Data sources
Official StartupTN communications; implementer materials (Villgro, Headstart); regulatory and corporate
records; the full text of cited third-party reports (notably the Primus Partners procurement report); and
named secondary reporting (Inc42, YourStory, Entrackr, Business Standard, The Statesman). All sources
are listed with links in References.

3.3 Evidence grading
Confirmed — supported by a primary source or by multiple named secondary sources.
Emerging — component and connection documented, but loop closure not yet evidenced.
A separate causal grade applies to any claim that a programme produced an outcome: Established
(counterfactual or strong independent mechanism), Asserted (claimed, often by the funder, without
counterfactual), or Not tested.

3.4 Verification protocol
Three rules, adopted after errors in the research process:
1. Verify the connection, not just the entity. A real company plus a real funding event does not establish
that the funding was caused by a programme; each causal link was checked against a source asserting
it directly.
2. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. Unverifiable claims were marked Emerging, not
false; several were later Confirmed once primary sources were consulted.
3. Relationship is not causation. Confirming that a startup is a programme beneficiary, even via the
programme’s own communications, confirms the relationship and the funder’s claim — not the
counterfactual. Funder-sourced causal claims are graded Asserted, not Established.

3.5 Limitations
There is no counterfactual in this study: no comparison group of similar non-funded startups, and no
discontinuity analysis around eligibility thresholds. Causal claims are therefore graded but not proven.
Aggregate figures (e.g., follow-on totals) are largely self-reported by StartupTN and unaudited here. A
substantial share of beneficiary-level causal claims rests on StartupTN’s own posts — the party with the
strongest incentive to claim credit. Several programmes are recent (2024–2026), so “Emerging” frequently
denotes youth; one exception is noted (TANSEED, §6.2). Distributional (ownership) data at founder/captable level is largely unavailable in public records, so §6.3’s distributional analysis is directional.

Chapter 4 — The Provisioning and Connection Layers
4.1 Provisioning (RQ1)
Tamil Nadu has an instrument for nearly every layer of the stack:
Talent: Naan Mudhalvan and the Niral Thiruvizha hackathon, reaching ~28,000 students across ~290
institutions in a recent edition [27].
Early capital: TANSEED grants (₹10–15 lakh); a dedicated SC/ST Startup Fund; a women-led track;
angel-network and investor-connect programmes; thematic funds [7], [8].
Market access: a procurement framework relaxing eligibility barriers, with direct purchase up to ₹50
lakh via TANSIM [24], [31].
Deep-tech anchoring: IIT Madras Research Park and the iTNT Hub [6], [28].
Convening: the annual Umagine TN summit [29].
RQ1 is answered affirmatively: provisioning is comprehensive and spans the stack.

4.2 Connection (RQ2)
Tamil Nadu’s design wires components together:
TANSEED takes a ~3% support stake (from edition five) and enrolls recipients in a year-long
accelerator with mentorship and delegations [8].
Niral Thiruvizha issues finalists Smart Cards routing them into hubs and incubators [27].
The SC/ST Fund invests via equity/CCDs, making the state a shareholder [22].
iTNT Hub connects 570+ colleges, researchers, and industry [28].
TN-RISE is a women founders’ council and co-creation centre with mentorship MoUs [20].
RQ2 is answered: connection is intentional and architecturally explicit.

Chapter 5 — Findings: Case Studies
Each case answers RQ3 (is recursion evidenced?) and, where relevant, RQ6 (caused or selected?).

5.1 The Zoho / Freshworks Founder Flywheel (benchmark)
Context. Zoho and its spin-out Freshworks made Chennai a leading SaaS hub with no decisive state role.
Provisioning/connection. Organic and cultural: a skills-first employer training engineers who later founded
companies. Recursion evidence. ~59 former Zoho employees became founders (Freshworks, Chargebee,
Facilio, etc.); alumni founders became angels and mentors [2], [3]. Loop status. Closed — every turn is
evidenced. Verification / causal grade. Confirmed; not state-caused (organic) [1], [2]. Assessment. Proof
recursion is achievable here — and the reference standard. But the loop is narrow (urban, English-educated
SaaS) and emerged organically over ~two decades, not via policy. It is the clearest case where recursion is
real and ownership remains concentrated among the already-advantaged.

5.2 IIT Madras Research Park / IITMIC (institutional anchor)
Context. India’s first university research park plus its incubation cell — the country’s leading deep-tech
incubator. Provisioning/connection. Lab access, faculty linkage, IP support, investor network, and an
incubation model that retains a stake in companies so founders stay engaged [5]. Recursion evidence.
457 startups over ~12 years; survival ~80% (vs typical 8–10%); ~60% non-IIT founders; two unicorns; an
IPO (Ather) [4], [6]. Loop status. Closed (institutional). Cap-table retention is itself a designed recursion
mechanism. Verification / causal grade. Confirmed; caused institutionally (not by StartupTN) [4], [5].
Assessment. A model for how an institution (not a market, and not the state) can manufacture recursion.
Inherited by the broader ecosystem.

5.3 TANSEED (the state’s flagship — relationship confirmed, causation not established)
Context. Launched 2021; StartupTN’s flagship early-capital instrument and the central test of whether the
state can build a loop. Provisioning/connection. ₹10–15 lakh grants; ~3% support stake; year-long
accelerator; routing into incubators and investors; ~169 startups supported since 2021 [8]. Recursion
evidence (aggregate). Against ~₹40 crore allocated, supported startups have collectively raised ~₹535
crore in external follow-on funding; named beneficiaries include Buyofuel, Ippopay, Gallabox, Dream
Aerospace, Torus Robotics, Plugzmart, Greenpod [7].
The causal caveat (central to this case). This evidence is co-occurrence, not a counterfactual. TANSEED
selects promising startups; its association with ₹535 crore of follow-on is as consistent with skilled
selection as with catalysis. Crucially, the celebrated cases are precisely those where selection dominates:
ippopay (Mohan K, Chennai; payments). 2021 TANSEED winner; founder calls it the company’s “first
recognition,” after which it scaled. Turns evidenced: grant → validation → scale. Causal grade: Asserted.
Confirmed relationship [9], [10].
PrimeInvestor (Srikanth Meenakshi, Vidya Bala, Bhavana Acharya; wealthtech). A ₹10 lakh TANSEED
3.0 grant in 2022; a ₹19.5 crore Rainmatter (Zerodha) round in 2026 — its first external round. The
founders are veteran ex-FundsIndia operators; a four-year-old non-dilutive grant is unlikely to have
caused a Zerodha-backed round. This is the clearest illustration that beneficiary status and later
success can be linked by the funder without a causal mechanism. Causal grade: Asserted (weak).
Relationship confirmed [11], [12], [13], [14].
REUDE Technologies (Reginald Chellam; drone/UAV). TANSEED 4.0 grantee and the sole TN finalist
among the top ten at Stanford’s Seed Spark — i.e., selected for unusual promise. Causal grade:
Asserted. Confirmed relationship [15], [16].
HARVTECH (agritech/EV-tractors). Selected from 1,000+ applicants for a TANSEED 4.0 grant; stacked
with IIT-M Carbon Zero recognition and a BIRAC BIG grant — again, selected for promise. Causal grade:
Asserted. Confirmed relationship [17], [18].
The cases that would demonstrate causation are the marginal ventures that would not have reached
prototype or pilot without ₹10–15 lakh and an accelerator — and those are exactly the ones not celebrated,
and for which no data is published.
Loop status. Selection vs treatment unresolved. The beneficiary relationships and the scaling are
Confirmed; that TANSEED caused the scaling is only Asserted. The grant → validation → scale arc is real;
whether the state catalysed it or merely identified it is undetermined — and the strongest evidence skews
toward identification. Assessment. TANSEED demonstrates that the state is a capable selector and

credentialer whose imprimatur co-occurs with private capital. That is a valuable ecosystem function — it
lowers VC search costs and confers legitimacy. It is not the same as originating the outcome, and the
catalytic claim remains unproven.

5.4 Public Procurement: Tamil Nadu vs. Rajasthan (comparative case)
Context. “Government as first customer” is a textbook recursion mechanism. Tamil Nadu has the policy; a
peer state shows the outcomes. Provisioning/connection. TN relaxes eligibility barriers and permits direct
procurement up to ₹50 lakh [24]. Recursion evidence (thin). The Primus Partners report (Table 6) records
Tamil Nadu at ~5 startups / ~₹0.54 crore in work orders — near Karnataka (₹0.49 crore) and Telangana (~₹1
crore) [24]. Contrast — Rajasthan. Via iStart and e-Bazaar (plus procurement-law reform), Rajasthan
issued 132 work orders worth over ₹25 crore to 476 startups; recipient surveys report majorities citing
improved market access and ~70% citing increased investor interest after government contracts [24], [25].
Kerala (~141 startups) is a second working model. Loop status. Open in Tamil Nadu; closed in Rajasthan.
Verification / causal grade. Confirmed figures; the Rajasthan loop has at least survey-level mechanism
evidence (Established–leaning) [24]. Assessment. The most actionable gap, and an instructive contrast:
this is the one case where a state can plausibly claim to have caused the loop (Rajasthan’s platform + legal
reform preceded the orders) — and Tamil Nadu has not built it.

5.5 SC/ST Startup Fund (an ownership-broadening instrument — still open)
Context. An exclusive equity fund for SC/ST founders; an explicit distributional instrument.
Provisioning/connection. ~₹60.8 crore across ~26+ startups (₹7.5 crore to five in batch one; ₹9.75 crore
to eight in batch two; ₹34.6 crore to 26 by end-2023), via equity/CCDs with post-investment mentorship
[22], [23]. Recursion evidence. Named, funded companies — Ecopmin Technologies (₹3 crore), Adhiban
Farms (₹2.2 crore), COEO Wellness (₹60 lakh), Mannvasanai Traditional Food (₹45 lakh), Nanjil Nadu Kani
Tribal Women Creations (₹25 lakh), TAMS Tribal Green Fuel [22], [23]. Loop status. Emerging. Capital and
credibility flow to founders; the aim of alumni becoming mentors/role models is not yet documented.
Assessment. Precisely the kind of instrument the ownership question cares about — and it has not closed.
The state’s distributional bets remain at the funding stage, not yet recursive.

5.6 Women-Led TANSEED and TN-RISE (an ownership-broadening instrument — still
open)
Context. A women-led TANSEED track plus TN-RISE, a women founders’ council launched July 2024
(World Bank-supported) [20], [21]. Provisioning/connection. ₹15 lakh women-led TANSEED awards; TNRISE as a co-creation centre with mentorship MoUs (Flipkart, HP, FLO) and a stated target of ~1,000+
women mentored per year [21]. (Some StartupTN materials cite larger targets; this thesis uses the
conservative figure.) Recursion evidence. At the Global Women Summit 2026 (TNWeSafe), ₹15 lakh
TANSEED awards went to women-led ventures including Dukil Medical and Technical Textiles (Santhiya
Selvakumar, Erode), Aruvi Eco (Kalaiselvi Balamurugan, Tiruppur), and others across Coimbatore, Chennai,
and Tiruvannamalai (StartupTN official post, Global Women Summit 2026). Loop status. Emerging.
Network and mentorship architecture exist; founder-to-founder peer financing/mentorship closure not
documented; TN-RISE is barely a year old. Assessment. Like the SC/ST fund, an ownership-broadening
instrument that has not yet closed — reinforcing the §6.3 pattern.

5.7 Niral Thiruvizha (talent funnel, pipeline documented)
Context. A mass student-innovation hackathon under Naan Mudhalvan [27]. Provisioning/connection.

~28,450 students from ~290 institutions; ₹10,000 prototype grants (top 1,000), ₹2 lakh vouchers (top 50),
Smart Cards routing finalists into hubs [27]. Recursion evidence. The pipeline is documented — e.g., KCG
College of Technology had two teams among the top 50 for ₹1 lakh grants (144 teams shortlisted from
837), connected to StartupTN regional hubs (KCG College official post, Sept 2024). Loop status.
Promising / open. Funnel and hand-off are real; a “hackathon team became funded company” exit is not
on record. Assessment. An excellent top-of-funnel whose recursion claim rests on conversion data the
programme does not publish.

Chapter 6 — Cross-Case Analysis and Discussion
6.1 The causation problem: selector, not originator (RQ6)
Across the closed and partly-closed cases, the state’s demonstrated role is identification and certification,
not origination. The two fully closed loops are inherited (Zoho organic; IITMRP institutional). The flagship
state instrument, TANSEED, shows beneficiary relationships and scaling but no counterfactual; its
showcase cases are selected-for-promise, so its headline ₹535 crore follow-on is at least as consistent
with skilled selection as with catalysis. The honest reframe is therefore stronger and less flattering than
“the first state-built loop is closing”: the state is best evidenced as a credentialer, convener, and
selector riding loops it does not clearly originate. This is not a dismissal — good selection and signalling
lower search costs for investors and confer legitimacy, which is real value — but it is a different and weaker
claim than catalysis, and it is the claim the evidence supports. The one place a state plausibly caused a
loop is Rajasthan’s procurement platform, which Tamil Nadu has not replicated.

6.2 The half-turn problem (RQ4)
Almost every confirmed state case demonstrates grant → validation → scale → follow-on capital and
stops there. The missing turn is founder → investor/mentor → next founder — the turn that converts
funded alumni into a self-sustaining flywheel, and the one turn the state can prompt but not provision. For
the newest programmes this absence is plausibly just youth. For TANSEED it is becoming weak data: five
years and eight editions in, with ~169 beneficiaries, there is still no documented case of a TANSEED
graduate financing or formally mentoring a later cohort. This is suggestive rather than conclusive — the full
flywheel historically takes ~15 years (Zoho’s did), and a founder must first reach liquidity — but at five years
the silence is no longer purely premature.

6.3 Recursion is not ownership (RQ7)
Every loop measured here closes through follow-on funding, which dilutes founders and channels equity to
external investors and acquirers. A vigorously recursing ecosystem can therefore be an ownershipconcentrating one. Two observations sharpen this:
1. The closed loop is narrow. The only fully closed loop (Zoho/Freshworks SaaS) is urban, Englisheducated, and elite. Recursion here is real and concentrated among the already-advantaged.
2. The broadening instruments are the open ones. The programmes explicitly designed to widen who
owns — the SC/ST fund, the women-led track, rural and tier-2 efforts — are precisely the loops that
have not closed (§§5.5–5.6).
So the state’s distributional ambitions sit exactly where recursion is weakest, while the recursion that exists

widens nothing. A closing loop among the advantaged is not ownership-broadening. (This analysis is
directional: founder/cap-table-level ownership data is largely unavailable publicly, so the claim is that
recursion and ownership must be held apart, not that concentration is quantified.)

6.4 Policy without a platform
Procurement shows a connection that exists on paper but lacks a transactional engine. Rajasthan’s contrast
indicates the decisive variable is operational infrastructure (e-Bazaar) plus legal reform, not policy intent
[24].

6.5 What predicts closure
Loops closed where (i) the connector had a structural reason to keep founders engaged (IITMRP’s captable retention; Zoho’s culture), (ii) a transactional platform converted eligibility into transactions
(Rajasthan’s e-Bazaar), and (iii) enough time had passed for a first generation to mature into financiers.
Tamil Nadu’s programmes have (i) partially (TANSEED’s stake), lack (ii) in procurement, and are still
accumulating (iii).

6.6 Source-reliability caveat
A large share of beneficiary-level causal claims rests on StartupTN’s own posts — the funder celebrating
beneficiaries it has every incentive to claim credit for. These are graded Confirmed for the relationship and
Asserted for causation. Independent corroboration exists for the beneficiary status of the key cases (e.g.,
the Headstart cohort listing for PrimeInvestor; the Stanford cohort page for REUDE) but not for the causal
link.

Chapter 7 — Conclusions and Recommendations
7.1 Conclusion (RQ5, RQ6, RQ7)
Tamil Nadu has understood that ecosystems are recursive and has engineered connection points into
almost every component it funds. Recursion is genuinely evidenced — but two qualifications reshape the
verdict. On causation: none of the closed or closing loops is clearly state-caused; the two fully closed
loops are inherited, and the flagship loop’s evidence is selection-consistent, so the state’s demonstrated
role is credentialer, convener, and selector rather than originator. On distribution: recursion is not
ownership; the one fully closed loop is narrow and elite, while the instruments built to broaden ownership
are exactly the ones still open.
The defensible verdict is therefore: ecosystem formation in Tamil Nadu is real and accelerating, but it is
neither clearly state-caused nor, as yet, ownership-widening. Recursion is necessary but not sufficient,
and closure among the already-advantaged is not the same as broad-based ownership. This is the
scorecard the state would not publish — which is the only kind worth writing.

7.2 Recommendations
1. Test causation, don’t assert it. Evaluate TANSEED against a comparison group of near-miss
applicants (a regression-discontinuity around the selection threshold) to separate selection from
treatment. Until then, claim credentialing, not catalysis.

2. Close the half-turn deliberately. Create pathways and incentives for TANSEED/SC/ST/women-led
alumni to become mentors and angels for later cohorts.
3. Give procurement a platform. Replicate Rajasthan’s e-Bazaar — a transactional catalog plus legal
reform, with published outcomes [24].
4. Measure ownership, not just formation. Publish founder-retention and cap-table-distribution data,
and track whether the SC/ST, women-led, rural, and tier-2 loops actually close — the test of whether
recursion is widening ownership or concentrating it.
5. Protect the institutional model. Treat IITMRP’s cap-table retention as a template for structurally
retaining founders.

7.3 Falsifiable indicators of genuine, broad-based closure (3–5 years)
A credible counterfactual evaluation showing TANSEED beneficiaries outperform comparable near-miss
applicants (causation, not selection).
Named cases of TANSEED/SC/ST/women-led graduates investing in or mentoring later cohorts (the
half-turn).
TN realized procurement work-order value rising from ~₹0.54 crore toward peer-state levels.
The SC/ST and women-led loops closing — beneficiaries recirculating — i.e., broadening loops, not only
the elite SaaS loop, beginning to recurse.
If these move, the ecosystem is becoming self-sustaining and broadening. If only the elite loop continues to
recurse while the rest stay open, Tamil Nadu will remain an impressively provisioned, deliberately
connected ecosystem whose recursion is narrow and whose self-sustenance is still state-credentialed.
