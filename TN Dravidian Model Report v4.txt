Central thesis: Tamil Nadu’s 2021–2026 experience suggests that social welfare and
economic transformation are not competing goals; they reinforce one another when
supported by fiscal capacity, state capacity, and industrial policy. This report assembles the
evidence, traces the institutional machinery, treats human capital as economic
infrastructure, and reads the period as the latest phase in a sixty-year developmental
trajectory.
Discipline note. Government projections, MoU commitments, contested figures, and
out-of-period data are flagged throughout. Credibility before advocacy.

2. Executive Summary
Between 2021 and 2026, four reinforcing goals — fiscal self-reliance, digital
administration, social security, climate-aligned infrastructure finance — underwrote a
simultaneous expansion of welfare and investment. Tamil Nadu remained India’s secondlargest state economy, grew fastest among states, doubled merchandise exports, and
became the leading electronics exporter, while deficits returned toward statutory norms.
The report’s distinctive claim is interpretive: 2021–2026 marks Tamil Nadu’s maturation
from a social-justice state and an industrial state into a developmental state, where
welfare and growth compound.

6. Creditworthiness & Fiscal Discipline
Fiscal deficit ~4.99% (2020-21 RE) → 3.0% (2025-26 BE); revenue deficit 3.28% → ~1.6%;
debt-to-GSDP ~26% within FC limits; outstanding debt ₹9.29→9.52 lakh cr. Absolute debt
rose, but the ratio held as GSDP grew — sustainability is about the ratio. (Indian states carry
no standalone rating; creditworthiness = deficit path + debt sustainability + market access.)

8. Industrial Depth as a Competitive Advantage
Tamil Nadu does not merely host factories — it hosts entire industrial ecosystems (OEMs
+ suppliers + logistics + skills co-located):
Automobile (“Detroit of India”): ~35% of India’s vehicle production; ~22 lakh auto
jobs; exports to 100+ countries.
Electronics: the cluster that made TN India’s No. 1 electronics exporter; the first State
Electronics Components Manufacturing Scheme deepens local value-add (PCBs,
displays, camera modules, sensors).
EV & batteries: an emerging hub on the auto base (Hosur–Krishnagiri belt).
Engineering, textiles, leather: No. 2 engineering exports; No. 1 textiles/leather —
diversified, formal-sector-heavy baskets.
GCCs & services: Chennai a leading Global Capability Centre and IT/fintech hub.
Ecosystems are sticky: suppliers, skills and logistics co-locate, raising the cost of exit and
the depth of value capture — which is why the position is durable, not just a list of
announcements.

PART IV — THE HUMAN-CAPITAL ENGINE
15. Human Capital as Economic Infrastructure
Tamil Nadu’s welfare schemes are not only social protection — they are labour-market

investments. Roads move goods; human capital moves economies.
CM Breakfast Scheme — nutrition that protects early-years cognitive development and
school attendance.
Pudhumai Penn & Tamil Pudhalvan — monthly stipends that keep government-school
students in higher education, widening the skilled-labour pool.
Naan Mudhalvan — a flagship skilling platform aligning students with industry-relevant
skills (the supply side of the manufacturing/services demand documented in Part II).
Higher-education expansion — among the highest gross enrolment ratios in India,
feeding engineering, electronics and services.
The argument: a deep industrial base (Part II) can only absorb labour if the workforce is
healthy, educated and skilled. Welfare spending on nutrition, schooling, stipends and skilling
is therefore an input to growth, building the human infrastructure that factories and GCCs
draw on — exactly the link the causal chain (§3) formalises. (To move this from plausible to
proven, pair it with district-level enrolment, skilling-placement and labour-forceparticipation data — see §24.)

PART V — INTERPRETATION & HISTORICAL
SIGNIFICANCE
18. The 2021–2026 Inflection Point
Was this a post-pandemic rebound, or the start of a transition into a high-technology
industrial economy? The evidence points to transition: electronics leadership +
component manufacturing (moving up the value chain), an EV/battery ecosystem,
semiconductor/advanced-electronics ambitions (early-stage), GCC/digital-services
growth, climate finance as a new asset class, and sustained manufacturing expansion.
Read together, the period looks less like recovery and more like a structural turning point.

19. An International Lens: Developmental-State Logic

Tamil Nadu’s combination of manufacturing + human capital + export orientation + an
active state echoes the logic of the East Asian developmental states — South Korea and
Taiwan, and more recently Vietnam — which paired export-led industrialisation with heavy
investment in education and health and deliberate industrial policy.
This is an analogy of logic, not a claim of equivalence. Important differences: Tamil Nadu is a
sub-national economy inside India’s federal and monetary framework — it does not control
currency, tariffs or sovereign trade policy, and its scale and per-capita income are different.
What the comparison illuminates is the development model: social investment and industrial
policy as complements, sustained over time, generating export competitiveness. It also
flags the same risks those economies faced — external-demand shocks (cf. the 2025
Tiruppur tariff episode), the need to keep climbing the value chain, and managing debt
during the build-out.

20. The Evolution of the Dravidian Model: 1967 → 2026
The 2021–2026 record is best understood as the third phase of a sixty-year trajectory.
Phase 1 — The Social-Justice State (1967 onward). Rooted in the Self-Respect and
Dravidian movements, the first DMK government (1967) institutionalised social justice:
broad reservation (eventually ~69%), expanded education access, the nutritious
midday-meal tradition (Kamaraj’s scheme, universalised under MGR), and Tamil language
and identity. The defining achievement was inclusion — widening who could participate in
public life.
Phase 2 — The Industrial State (c. 1990–2020). Liberalisation-era industrialisation
layered an economic base onto the social one: SIPCOT parks and industrial estates, the
automobile cluster (the “Detroit of India”), the rise of IT and services in Chennai, and a
dense MSME network. The defining achievement was capacity — building the factories,
corridors and firms.
Phase 3 — The Developmental State (2021–2026). This report’s subject: welfare and
human capital and digital governance and exports and advanced manufacturing and climate
finance, knit together by fiscal self-reliance and state capacity. The defining achievement is
synthesis — making distribution and production reinforce each other.
The interpretation: The Dravidian Model evolved from a social-justice project into a
developmental-state project without abandoning its social-justice foundations. Each
phase kept the previous one’s gains and added a new capability — inclusion, then
capacity, then synthesis.

21. From Welfare State to Developmental State (Capstone)

Everything above supports one proposition:
Social welfare and economic transformation are not competing goals; they can
reinforce one another when supported by strong institutions, fiscal capacity, and
industrial policy.
A welfare state distributes. A developmental state distributes while building productive
capacity — financing the social floor from its own revenue, delivering it through digital state
capacity, converting it into human capital, and channelling that human capital into a deep
industrial and export base. The Dravidian Model’s claim to significance is not any single
scheme or statistic; it is the demonstration that the two can compound. That is the
proposition worth citing in any serious discussion of Tamil Nadu’s development trajectory.

PART VI — FOR POLICY MAKERS
22. Observations & Recommendations
1. Publish revenue outcomes — an SGST/SOTR growth annexure to make the 75% claim
self-evident.
2. Lock in self-reliance structurally — finish Transport/Registration/TANGEDCO/GST
integration; quarterly receipts dashboard.
3. Manage the debt narrative — absolute debt up, ratio within limits; pair with the deficit
path.
4. Pre-fund social-security schemes — actuarial corpus rules (the Family Security Fund
needed a ₹121 cr bailout).
5. Report realised investment — a “committed → grounded → operational” scoreboard
for the ₹12.16 lakh crore.
6. Scale climate funds toward the 2070 target with measured carbon outcomes.
7. Track CAMS/APMS outcomes — closure rates, recoveries, time-to-resolution.
8. Be precise on rankings (EPI No.1 2022, No.2 2024).
9. Prove the human-capital thesis with micro-data — district-level enrolment, Naan
Mudhalvan placement rates, and female labour-force participation would convert §15–16
from plausible to proven.
10. Build resilience into the export model — the 2025 tariff shock shows the need for

market diversification and value-chain upgrading.
