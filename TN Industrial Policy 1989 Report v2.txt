1. Executive Summary
When the DMK government returned to power in 1989 under Chief Minister M. Karunanidhi, Tamil Nadu's industrial sector had been largely stagnant since 1977. The new administration moved with remarkable speed: within a month of taking office, the government convened consultations with entrepreneurs and industrialists, formulated a comprehensive industrial policy, and presented it to the Legislative Assembly on April 21, 1989.

This report analyses that policy document — presented as Annexure III to the Assembly — and situates it within Tamil Nadu's broader industrial trajectory. The 1989 policy is significant not merely as a catalogue of schemes, but as a state-capacity-building exercise that created the institutional foundations Tamil Nadu would draw upon when economic liberalisation arrived in 1991.

Central Argument
The 1989 Industrial Policy was not a liberalisation-era investment promotion policy. It was a state-capacity-building policy. By strengthening institutions — DICs, SIDCO, TIIC, cooperatives, and sector-specific support agencies — it created the industrial ecosystem that allowed Tamil Nadu to emerge as one of India's most industrialised states after 1991.

2. Why This Policy Matters — Historical Significance
The 1989 Tamil Nadu Industrial Policy deserves attention beyond its immediate operational details. It occupies a distinctive place in the state's industrial history for several interconnected reasons.

2.1 The Last Major Policy Before Liberalisation
The 1989 policy was the last comprehensive industrial policy Tamil Nadu formulated before the 1991 Union Budget and the consequent dismantling of the License Raj. It was therefore conceived, designed, and implemented entirely within a controlled-economy framework — where the state government's role was to be the primary promoter, financier, and infrastructure provider for industrial development. Understanding this policy is essential to understanding what the state brought to the table when liberalisation arrived.

2.2 Speed as a Governance Signal
The policy was formulated within weeks of the DMK assuming office. This was not administrative routine. It was a deliberate signal — to the investment community, to entrepreneurs, and to the public — that the new government regarded industrial development as an immediate priority. In an era before investment summits and global roadshows, the speed of policy formulation was itself an instrument of industrial promotion.

2.3 First Comprehensive Integration of Five Pillars
The 1989 policy achieved something that earlier Tamil Nadu industrial documents had not done with equal comprehensiveness: the simultaneous integration of five distinct pillars into a unified framework.

	•	Infrastructure: SIDCO's estate development programme, factory sheds, and the Japan ODA hi-tech cluster initiative
	•	Finance: TIIC's tiered lending structure, DIC seed capital, and multiple central-scheme facilitation
	•	Technology: Dedicated electronics estates, production approval streamlining, and quality certification infrastructure
	•	Cooperatives: Sector-specific cooperative frameworks for tea, sago, matchbox, coir, and other industries
	•	Entrepreneurship: PMRY, artisan schemes, women entrepreneur finance, and engineering graduate incentives

Earlier policies had addressed some of these pillars in isolation. The 1989 framework treated them as an interconnected system — a precondition for sustainable industrial growth.

2.4 Positioning Tamil Nadu for Post-1991 Growth
When economic liberalisation arrived in 1991, Tamil Nadu was unusually well-positioned to benefit. It already had a functioning network of industrial estates, a creditworthy state financial institution (TIIC), a large cooperative industrial sector, and a cadre of trained technical entrepreneurs. None of this was an accident — it was the product of deliberate institutional investment over decades, accelerated by the 1989 policy. The auto corridor, electronics manufacturing boom, and pharmaceutical cluster growth of the 1990s and 2000s were not created by liberalisation; they were enabled by it, on a foundation built earlier.

2.5 Early Focus on Sectors That Would Define Tamil Nadu's Future
The explicit priority given in 1989 to electronics, pharmaceuticals, and motor vehicle ancillaries — through dedicated industrial estates, higher subsidies, and Japan ODA financing — is striking in retrospect. These are precisely the three sectors that would come to define Tamil Nadu's industrial identity in the 21st century. The 1989 policy makers were, in effect, placing strategic bets on the future of the state economy.


3. Historical Context and Industrial Timeline
3.1 The Industrial Stagnation of 1977-1989
Tamil Nadu's industrial growth was significantly constrained during 1977-1989. Political instability translated into policy inertia. New investment was hesitant, and institutional frameworks for small industry promotion lacked fresh direction. By 1989, the accumulated backlog of industrial development had become a pressing economic priority — particularly in rural areas where non-farm employment was scarce.

4. Kalaignar's Industrial Development Model
The 1989 policy is not a neutral administrative document. It reflects a coherent set of political-economic values. Reading across its provisions, five recurring principles emerge that together constitute what might be called Kalaignar's industrial development model.

4.1 Industrialisation Beyond Chennai
The explicit preference for backward taluks, the provision of higher subsidies for non-Chennai industrial locations, the deliberate spread of DICs to every district, and the targeting of rural cooperatives all reflect a fundamental commitment: Tamil Nadu's industrial development should not be confined to the Chennai metropolitan corridor. The electronics estates were established in Hosur, Trichy, Madurai, and Coimbatore — not only Chennai. The 1989 policy was, among other things, a spatial equity programme.

4.2 Support for First-Generation Entrepreneurs
Multiple provisions — the artisan scheme, engineering graduate incentive, PMRY, TIIC's soft loans for technical entrepreneurs, and SIDCO's ready-built factory sheds at minimal rent — were specifically designed to lower the barriers for people starting businesses without inherited capital or established networks. This reflected a social democratic reading of industrial policy: that the primary constraint for aspiring entrepreneurs from modest backgrounds was not capability but access to infrastructure, finance, and market linkages.

4.3 Technology Adoption as a State Responsibility
The 1989 policy treated technology transfer and quality infrastructure as public goods that the state must provision. Testing laboratories, quality certification centres, the Scientific Glass Training Institute, the Ceramics Technology Training Institute, and the Electronic Development and Training Centre were all state investments in the productive capacity of private small businesses. The Japan ODA negotiation for hi-tech industrial estates was the most ambitious expression of this principle.

4.4 Regional Balance Through Industrial Dispersal
The backward area subsidy system, the coal distribution network (with storage depots in Madurai, Trichy, Salem, Vellore, and Coimbatore), the district-level DIC network, and the cooperative society structure in the Nilgiris, Salem, Dharmapuri, and Sivakasi belts all reflect a deliberate strategy of geographic dispersal. Industrial development was to be a vehicle for regional equity, not a reinforcer of existing spatial hierarchies.

4.5 Social Justice Through Industrial Policy
Perhaps the most distinctive feature of the 1989 framework is its systematic embedding of social justice into every level of the incentive architecture:

	•	90% of PMRY approvals reserved for SC/ST beneficiaries
	•	Dedicated financial windows for women entrepreneurs (12.5% interest rate vs. standard)
	•	Lower equity contribution requirements for tribal and Dalit entrepreneurs (10% vs. standard 25%)
	•	Matchbox cooperatives established specifically for below-poverty-line rural women
	•	Coir industry cooperatives targeted at coastal communities
	•	TALCO's leather programme designed to assist artisan communities historically engaged in the trade

These were not cosmetic additions. They were structural features of the incentive design, reflecting a conviction that industrial policy was inseparable from social policy.

5.1 DICs as Precursors to Single-Window Governance
The 1989 policy did not establish a formal single-window clearance mechanism in the contemporary statutory sense. However, District Industries Centres functioned as single-point facilitation agencies — helping entrepreneurs navigate approvals, registrations, statutory clearances, and incentive access. This is historically significant: it represents the organisational and procedural precursor to the formal single-window investment facilitation systems that Tamil Nadu and other states would introduce in the 1990s and 2000s.

As of January 31, 1989, DICs had already resolved 1,848 pending cases through task-force teams, demonstrating that the facilitation model was producing measurable results. The expanded DIC mandate under the 1989 policy — taking on responsibility for incentive disbursement, bank credit coordination, and sick-unit revival — effectively made each DIC a district-level industrial development authority, decades before that terminology entered common use.

7. Long-Term Finance: TIIC
7.1 Overview
TIIC (established 1949) is India's oldest state-level industrial finance institution. It provides term loans of Rs. 5,000 to Rs. 60 Lakh for fixed assets. Over the decade preceding the 1989 policy, TIIC had shifted 99% of its sanctions to SSI units — a deliberate portfolio reorientation toward small enterprise development.

8. SIDCO — Incubator of First-Generation Entrepreneurs
8.1 The Infrastructure Argument
Tamil Nadu's industrialisation was not driven solely by large corporations or established business families. The state's ability to generate a broad base of small manufacturers — across textiles, electronics, engineering, chemicals, leather, and food processing — owed much to a structural feature that is often underappreciated: the availability of ready-built, affordable industrial infrastructure through SIDCO's estate programme.

For a first-generation entrepreneur from a non-business background — perhaps a trained engineer, an artisan, or a technically skilled worker — the cost of acquiring land, constructing a factory shed, and installing utilities before a single unit could be produced was often prohibitive. SIDCO's industrial estates eliminated this barrier. Factory sheds were available on a cash basis at standardised rates. Essential utilities — power, water, drainage — were already installed. The entrepreneur could focus capital on machinery and working capital rather than on land and construction.

Beyond infrastructure, SIDCO served as a procurement and distribution agency for scarce industrial inputs — iron, steel, paraffin wax, potassium chlorate, zinc, and chemicals. In 1988-89 (April-January), SIDCO supplied 34,768 metric tons of raw materials valued at Rs. 3,552.54 Lakh to small industries. This function — reducing small producers' vulnerability to commodity shortages and price manipulation — was as important as the physical infrastructure role.

Tamil Nadu's distinctiveness in the late 1980s lay in three areas: (1) the depth and age of its state financial institution (TIIC, the oldest in India); (2) the breadth and social-equity orientation of its cooperative industrial sector; and (3) the early, deliberate focus on technology-intensive sectors through dedicated infrastructure. Maharashtra had scale; Gujarat had commercial dynamism; but Tamil Nadu had institutional depth across the full range of SSI support functions.

11.2 Leather Industry
TALCO (est. March 1983, Rs. 10 Crore authorised capital) addressed two structural challenges: upgrading small tanners and treating industrial effluents. The policy included a 10% additional capital subsidy specifically for leather tannery effluent treatment plants — an early integration of environmental compliance into industrial incentive design.

11.3 Ceramics Industry (Vridhachalam Cluster)
Three integrated facilities: a technology training institute (3-year diploma for 10th pass students, reduced from 4 years in 1988), a body-slip production centre (130-140 MT/month for 100 nearby SSIs), and a tunnel kiln providing round-the-clock firing services. By January 1989, the service centre had produced 1,352.58 MT of shaped articles (Rs. 15.49 Lakh) and heat-treated 1,085.59 MT of ceramic goods (Rs. 290.12 Lakh).

11.4 Chemicals, Salt, and Scientific Glass
Testing laboratories in Guindy, Madurai, and Coimbatore served 5,000+ industrial units annually. The Thoothukudi Marine Chemicals and Salt Research Station provided testing for coastal-chemical producers. A Scientific Glass Training Centre at Sandy trained 25 students per year for laboratory glassware manufacturing — a niche but strategically important programme.

13. Legacy and Long-Term Impact
The 1989 policy's legacy is best understood not through the immediate statistics it generated — registrations, loan sanctions, shed constructions — but through the institutional capacity it built and the industrial trajectories it enabled.

13.1 Auto Ancillary Growth (1990s-2000s)
The 1989 policy's explicit priority for motor vehicle ancillary industries, the dedicated electronics-plus-auto cluster strategy at Hosur, and the DIC network's penetration into districts like Krishnagiri and Vellore created conditions for the emergence of the Chennai-Hosur auto corridor that would become one of India's largest automotive manufacturing zones. The Hyundai plant at Sriperumbudur (1998), the Ford plant at Maraimalai Nagar (1998), and the hundreds of tier-1 and tier-2 ancillary suppliers that followed were investing into a state that already had industrial infrastructure, a trained workforce, and a functioning financial ecosystem.

13.2 Electronics Manufacturing Surge
The electronics estates established under the 1989 framework — particularly Perungudi and the planned hi-tech clusters — were direct precursors to the Sriperumbudur electronics corridor (Foxconn, Samsung, Flex, and others). TIDEL Park (1997), Tamil Nadu's first IT park, explicitly adopted the single-estate model pioneered by SIDCO. The continuity of institutional form across three decades is striking.

13.3 Pharmaceutical Cluster
The special capital subsidy for pharmaceutical industries and the inclusion of pharma in the Japan ODA hi-tech estate programme seeded what would become a significant pharmaceutical manufacturing cluster in Tamil Nadu, particularly in the Chennai-Chengalpattu belt and around Baddi-type zones.

13.4 Rural Industrialisation and Cooperative Legacy
The tea cooperatives in the Nilgiris, the sago cooperatives in Salem and Dharmapuri, the matchbox cooperatives in Sivakasi, and the coir cooperatives in coastal districts all continued operating and in many cases expanding after 1989. More importantly, they established an organisational template — commodity-based cooperatives with apex federations for marketing and procurement — that influenced cooperative policy in Tamil Nadu for decades.

13.5 Evolution Toward Modern Investment Facilitation
The DIC-as-single-point-facilitator model of 1989 directly informed the Guidance Bureau (1992), Tamil Nadu's dedicated investment promotion agency, and subsequently the single-window clearance system formalised in the Tamil Nadu Transparency in Tenders Act and related regulations. The institutional logic — reduce entrepreneur friction through a dedicated facilitation authority — was continuous even as the forms evolved.

The Central Legacy
Liberalisation accelerated Tamil Nadu's industrial growth, but it did not create it. The institutional architecture — DICs, SIDCO, TIIC, sector cooperatives, testing laboratories, and technology estates — was already in place. The 1989 policy was the most complete articulation of that architecture before market forces took over as the primary driver of industrial location decisions.


14. Assessment — Strengths, Gaps, and Lessons
14.1 Strengths
	•	Comprehensiveness: Full value chain coverage — infrastructure, raw materials, finance, technology, marketing
	•	Speed and decisiveness: One-month policy formulation communicated governance credibility
	•	Social equity integration: Reserved categories, women, and backward areas embedded structurally, not cosmetically
	•	Technology forward: Electronics estates and Japan ODA demonstrated early recognition of technology-led growth
	•	Cooperative depth: Commodity cooperatives with apex federations provided market power to small producers
	•	Institutional continuity: Building on TIIC (1949) and SIDCO (1971) rather than creating new institutions

14.2 Challenges and Gaps
	•	Coal/coke logistics: Railway wagon shortage — beyond state control — was disrupting SSI raw material supply
	•	Import dependency: Iron, steel, and alloy shortages required central advocacy; state capacity was limited
	•	PMRY declining trend: Approvals fell from 22,162 (1983-84) to 4,602 (1988-89) — a 79% decline requiring explanation
	•	Cooperative viability: 100 societies dissolved in two years alongside expansion — structural viability challenges persisted
	•	Limited technology transfer mechanism: The Japan ODA programme notwithstanding, mechanisms for importing and adapting technology into SSIs were underdeveloped
