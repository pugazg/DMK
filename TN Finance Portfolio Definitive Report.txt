B. Executive Summary
Between 2021 and 2026 the Finance portfolio pursued four reinforcing goals — fiscal selfreliance, digital administration, social security, and climate-aligned infrastructure
finance — which together underwrote a simultaneous expansion of welfare and investment.
The macroeconomic outcome was strong: Tamil Nadu retained its place as India’s secondlargest state economy, grew fastest among states in 2024-25, doubled merchandise
exports, and became the country’s leading electronics exporter. The fiscal outcome was a
credible recovery from the pandemic shock — deficits brought back toward statutory norms
while own-revenue mobilisation rose to ~75% of revenue receipts.
The institutional story beneath these numbers is the report’s distinctive contribution: a
treasury and HR system (IFHRMS 2.0) capable of disbursing salaries, pensions and scheme
benefits at scale; a unified audit apparatus (CAMS/APMS); catalytic fund vehicles
(TNIFMC) that supplement budget spending; and a climate and heritage agenda treated as
economic policy rather than peripheral activity.

C. Economic Transformation (2021–2026)
This chapter responds to the most-asked question — what did the economy actually do —
using externally verified official data.

C.1 Growth and Size
GSDP (current prices): approximately ₹17.9 lakh crore (2020-21) to ₹31.19 lakh
crore (2024-25); the Budget projects ~₹36.57 lakh crore (2025-26 BE). (The 202425 figure and growth rate are from the Economic Survey of Tamil Nadu 2025-26; the
2020-21 starting figure is per the source note and is broadly consistent with published
series.)
Real GSDP growth of 11.19% in 2024-25 — the highest among Indian states and the
state’s first double-digit growth in 14 years; nominal growth ~16%.
Tamil Nadu remains India’s second-largest state economy (after Maharashtra),
contributing roughly 9% of national GDP with ~4% of land area.
The State has set a trillion-dollar-economy aspiration (variously cited as 2030 or
2032-33).

C.2 Incomes and Living Standards
Per-capita income reached ₹3.62 lakh in 2024-25, about 1.77× the national
average (~₹2.05 lakh), up from ₹3.13 lakh in 2023-24.
32 of 38 districts recorded per-capita incomes above the all-India average.
Multidimensional poverty (National MPI) fell from 4.76% (2015-16) to 2.20% (201921), with more recent estimates near ~1.4%. Caveat: the bulk of this decline occurred in
2015-16 to 2019-21, largely predating the 2021-26 tenure; it reflects a long-run
trajectory rather than a single-term outcome.

C.3 Exports and Manufacturing — the Growth Engine
Merchandise exports nearly doubled from $26.15 billion (2020-21) to $52.07 billion
(2024-25).
Tamil Nadu ranks first nationally in electronics, textiles and leather exports, and
second in engineering goods.
Electronics exports surged from $1.66 billion (2021-22) to a record $14.65 billion
(2024-25) — making Tamil Nadu India’s top electronics-exporting state, and the first
to launch a State Electronics Components Manufacturing Scheme.
Manufacturing grew 14.74% and services 11.3% in 2024-25; the secondary sector
contributes ~33% of GSDP.
FDI inflows rose to $3.68 billion (2024-25) from ~$2.17 billion (2022-23), defying the
national slowdown.

C.4 Export Readiness — a Precise Statement
Tamil Nadu was ranked No. 1 in NITI Aayog’s Export Preparedness Index (EPI) 2022. In
the most recent EPI 2024 (released January 2026), Maharashtra overtook it and Tamil
Nadu ranked second among large states (with Gujarat third). The State therefore remains a
consistent top-tier exporter, while no longer holding the No. 1 EPI slot — an honest framing
that strengthens, rather than weakens, the report’s credibility.
Context (independent view): An analytical column (Mudar Patherya) estimated Tamil
Nadu’s nominal-dollar GSDP rising from ~$241 billion (2020-21) to ~$369 billion (202425) — about $128 billion of wealth creation, ~$32 billion a year — characterised as
outperformance. The author cautions these are indicative nominal-dollar figures
influenced by inherited momentum and external factors; treat as directional.

Benchmark takeaway: Tamil Nadu funds about three-quarters of its revenue from its own

D.2 Why This Matters
Self-reliance is the fiscal precondition for the “Dravidian Model”: large recurring welfare
commitments (free bus travel, women’s income support, breakfast scheme) are only
sustainable if the State controls a dependable, growing own-revenue base. The treasury,
GST and e-receipt reforms (Chapters F and J) are precisely what protect that base.

F. GST & Revenue Mobilisation — the Engine of the 75% Ratio
The high own-revenue ratio (Chapter D) is the outcome; this chapter is the cause.
State Own Tax Revenue is anchored by State GST and Commercial Taxes, supported
by stamps & registration, state excise, vehicle tax and electricity duties.
Modernisation levers documented in the departmental note: e-Challan/e-Receipt
24×7 online payment of dues; RBI NEFT/RTGS for instant receipt credit (since April

2023); integration of Commercial Taxes and Prohibition & Excise into the receipts
platform; four aggregator banks sweeping revenue into the State’s RBI account.
Why it is remarkable: sustaining a ~75% own-revenue share while expanding welfare
requires both buoyant tax collections and leak-proof, real-time receipt systems. The
digital receipt architecture is what converts economic growth (Chapter C) into usable
fiscal space.
Data gap to close before external use: the departmental note describes integration but
not collection outcomes. A short annexure with SOTR and SGST collection growth
(2020-21 to 2025-26) would make this chapter authoritative.

I. Climate Finance Leadership
Tamil Nadu is among the few Indian states attempting climate governance, climate
finance and green industrialisation as a single coordinated strategy — which is why this
deserves flagship status rather than a regulatory footnote.
TN Green Climate Fund (TNGCF): SEBI-registered (03.08.2023); first phase ₹110
crore across three investments; target 2.5 million MT carbon reduction, aligned to the
State’s net-zero-by-2070 pathway.
TN Emerging Sector Seed Fund (TNESSF): 13 startups, ₹30.1 crore committed;
targeted impact includes 1,500+ EVs, carbon/material-use reduction and ~60,000 jobs.
District Climate Change Missions in every district (Collector as Project Director, DFO
as Climate Officer); Green Local Bodies (297 selected); Green Fellows running 1,284+
awareness campaigns reaching ~36 lakh people.

Meendum Manjappai: 15 lakh+ cloth bags, 200+ dispensing machines, 2.99 lakh
awareness events, films broadcast to 60,000 schools.
Pollution control with measurable results (TNPCB): NCAP-period PM10 reductions of
Thoothukudi −45%, Trichy −18%, Madurai −18%, Chennai −12% (2021–2025); 55
continuous air-quality and 8 real-time water-quality stations; NABL-accredited labs.
Why this is unusual: climate is treated as an economic portfolio — fund vehicles,
industrial incentives and infrastructure pipelines — not merely compliance. It is
administratively co-located with Finance, reinforcing the link to long-term infrastructure
financing.

J. Treasury & Accounts Department (Operational Detail)
J.1 Mandate and Scale
Reorganised as a separate department in 1962 under Finance; operates 6 zonal JointDirector offices, 6 Pay & Accounts Offices, 3 Sub-Pay & Accounts Offices, 38 District
Treasuries, 1 Pension Pay Office and 243 Sub-Treasuries. It salaries 9.2 lakh+ employees,
pays 7.4 lakh+ pensioners, releases scheme funds, sends monthly accounts to the AG, and
administers stamps and medical insurance.

J.2 IFHRMS 2.0 — Features
Statewide (piloted in Karur, Jan 2020); salaries/bills via RBI e-Kuber; e-Challan/e-Receipt
(24×7), four aggregator banks; NEFT/RTGS since 24.04.2023; Kaniniyam app
(27.02.2024). Modules: OPPAS (e-pension/gratuity/GPF settlement), DAAS (paperless eVouchers; AG exemption from 01.02.2025), automatic income-tax computation, CPS final
settlement, NPS-to-NSDL, and duplicate-beneficiary detection (CMUPT, OAP, SSN-IG).
Ongoing integration: Transport, Registration, TANGEDCO (phased from 01.01.2025), BSNL,
and a Global Beneficiary Master with large-batch (up to 10 lakh) disbursal.

J.3 Workforce Social Security
Pensioners’ Family Security Fund: ₹50,000 lump-sum on death; ₹121 crore advanced
to clear a COVID-era backlog — 25,000 claims/families settled. (A source sidebar cites
₹125 crore — see Caveats.)
Employees’ Medical Insurance (2021): ₹5 lakh cover (special-disease ceiling up to
₹20 lakh), ₹300/month, 203 procedures, 1,497 hospitals + 16 radiation centres;
extended to 30.06.2026. Outcome: 3,33,675 beneficiaries, ₹1,706.19 crore.

children; ₹10 lakh life cover for natural death. ₹20.65 crore paid to 46 families to date.

K. Audit Reforms
Unified Chief Auditor office created to bring Local Fund, Co-operative, Milk Cooperative, State Government and HR&CE audit under Finance.

Milk co-operatives: a record 53,280 primary societies audited; performance audit
introduced (2023) for charitable institutions; Template Audit for ~18,000 small bodies.

L.2 TNIDB, Investment Attraction and the GIM Linkage
The strongest political-economy argument is that Finance and Industry worked together
— fiscal stability underwriting investor confidence.
Cumulative tenure performance (2021–2026): Tamil Nadu signed 1,176+ investment
MoUs worth ₹12.16 lakh crore (~$136 billion), with an employment potential of ~36.4
lakh jobs (direct + indirect); the Industries Department reports ~80% of MoUs under
execution (a later “Conversion Conclave 2026” update cited ₹12.37 lakh crore / 1,179
MoUs / 36.52 lakh jobs and a 73.5% conversion rate). These are committed/underexecution figures, not fully realised investment — the execution and conversion rates
are the metrics to track.
Global Investors Meet 2024 (7–8 January 2024): ₹6.64 lakh crore committed,
expected to create 26.9 lakh jobs (14.55 lakh direct + 12.36 lakh indirect) — the single
largest mobilisation event within the cumulative total above.
TNIDB runs a large PPP/HAM/DBFOT pipeline — water (desalination, smart meters),
energy (battery storage, pumped hydro, rooftop solar), mobility (electric buses, ECR
elevated corridor) and industrial parks.
The State frames itself as “India’s manufacturing capital”, targeting a $1 trillion
economy by 2030.

Chennai City Partnership: Sustainable Urban Services Program — US$701 million total,
with World Bank and AIIB contributing US$150 million each; 19 disbursement-linked
indicators; TNIDB as monitoring agency.

M. Heritage as Economic Policy
A distinctively Tamil Nadu feature — archaeology framed as tourism, identity and local
livelihood, not merely culture.
₹16.93 crore conservation (Thanjavur, Danish Fort/Tharangambadi, Madurai Thirumalai
Nayakkar Palace); 2025-26 allocation ₹25 crore.
Keeladi world-class museum (₹3.8 crore, 34 posts); Porunai Museum, Tirunelveli
(₹58.69 crore).
Scientific milestones: Mayiladumparai iron artefacts dated to ~4,200 years (c. 2172
BCE), advancing India’s Iron-Age chronology; Gangaikonda Cholapuram excavations
revealing Chola maritime trade.
20,000+ inscriptions documented; four new district museums (Tirunelveli,
Ramanathapuram, Tenkasi, Kanyakumari); MoUs with foreign universities; student
stipend raised ₹4,000 → ₹5,000.
Economic logic: sites like Keeladi generate local employment and domestic/foreign
tourist footfall, converting heritage into regional economic activity.

N. Synthesis — How Tamil Nadu Financed the Dravidian Model
If the welfare schemes are the visible output, this report documents the institutional
architecture beneath them. Six pillars operated simultaneously:
1. Fiscal self-reliance (~75% own revenue) created sustainable budget space.
2. Digital state capacity (IFHRMS 2.0, e-Challan, CAMS/APMS, TNPFMS) made delivery
and revenue capture dependable.
3. Welfare at scale rode on those rails (9.2 lakh employees, 7.4 lakh pensioners, 14 lakh +
8 lakh scheme beneficiaries).
4. Infrastructure finance (TNIFMC funds + PPP pipeline + GIM 2024) supplemented
budget spending with instruments and private capital.
5. Climate-aligned planning tied green funds and industrialisation to a 2070 net-zero
pathway.
6. Economic transformation followed — fastest-growing state, No. 1 electronics exporter,
doubled merchandise exports, rising per-capita income.
The complete statement: Tamil Nadu’s 2021–2026 model combined large-scale welfare

expansion with fiscal self-reliance, digital governance, audit and treasury reform,
infrastructure finance, climate planning, and export-oriented industrial growth — a more
complete explanation of the “Dravidian Model” than any single scheme or statistic conveys.
It is, in effect, a case study in state capacity and economic governance.

O. Observations & Recommendations for Policy Makers
1. Publish the revenue outcomes. Add an annexure with SGST/SOTR and commercial-tax
collection growth (2020-21→2025-26) to make the 75% own-revenue claim selfevidently authoritative (Chapter F gap).
2. Lock in self-reliance structurally, not cyclically — complete
Transport/Registration/TANGEDCO/GST data integration and publish a quarterly receipts
dashboard.
3. Manage the debt narrative proactively. State that absolute debt has risen but the
debt-to-GSDP ratio remains within limits; pair this with the deficit-reduction path to preempt criticism.
4. Pre-fund social-security schemes. The Family Security Fund needed a ₹121 crore
bailout; an actuarial corpus rule would prevent recurrence (apply equally to the medicalinsurance corpus).
5. Report realised PPP/GIM investment, not just commitments — convert the ₹6.64 lakh
crore GIM headline into a tracked “committed vs. grounded vs. operational” scoreboard.
6. Scale the climate funds. TNGCF/TNESSF are early-stage relative to the Shelter Fund;
clear deal pipelines and measured carbon outcomes would build them toward the 2070
target.
7. Track CAMS/APMS outcomes (closure rates, recoveries, time-to-resolution) so
digitisation translates into demonstrable accountability.
8. Be precise on rankings (e.g., EPI No. 1 in 2022, No. 2 in 2024) — accuracy protects the
report’s authority with legislators and rating-sensitive audiences.

checked against the Economic Survey of Tamil Nadu 2025-26, Tamil Nadu Budget

Attribution discipline:
Poverty decline (4.76%→2.20%) occurred mainly in 2015-16 to 2019-21, largely
before the tenure — do not over-attribute.
Export Preparedness Index: No. 1 in 2022; No. 2 (behind Maharashtra) in EPI
2024, released January 2026.
Patherya GSDP-wealth figures are an independent nominal-dollar estimate with
the author’s own caveats — directional only.
Investment figures (₹12.16 lakh crore; GIM 2024 ₹6.64 lakh crore) are
committed MoU values, externally corroborated via the Industries Department; they
are not fully realised investment — track execution (~80%) and conversion (~73.5%)
rates.
No standalone credit rating exists for Indian states; “creditworthiness” is
expressed via deficits, debt-to-GSDP and market-borrowing access.
Source inconsistencies in the departmental note: Family Security Fund advance cited
as ₹125 crore (sidebar) vs ₹121 crore (body); several treasury-building costs appear
mis-transcribed (likely crore, not lakh) and dubious values were omitted; medicalinsurance cover read as ₹5/10/20 lakh; the TNIDB “2030 economy target” figure was
corrupted in the extraction and is described qualitatively; hospital counts differ slightly
(1,497 vs 1,490) between the employee and pensioner sections.
Currency: figures are in Indian rupees (crore/lakh) unless a US-dollar figure is explicitly
stated. Verify all headline numbers against original budget documents before external
citation.
