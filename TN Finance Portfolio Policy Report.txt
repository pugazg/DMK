1. Executive Summary
Over the review period the Finance portfolio has pursued four reinforcing goals: fiscal selfreliance, digital administration, social security for the government workforce, and
climate-aligned infrastructure financing.
Strong own-revenue position. Tamil Nadu finances a large share of its expenditure
from its own revenue — rising from roughly 69% of total revenue receipts in 2015-16 to
75.3% (2025-26 BE), against an all-states average of only 58.2%. The State sits firmly
in the top tier of fiscally self-reliant states.
Digital backbone. The Integrated Financial and Human Resource Management
System (IFHRMS 2.0 / “Kaniniyam”) now drives salary, pension, scheme transfers and
revenue receipts end-to-end, integrated with RBI’s e-Kuber and with major revenue
departments.
Workforce social security at scale. Medical-insurance cover for ~9.2 lakh employees
and ~7.4 lakh pensioners, a refreshed Pensioners’ Family Security Fund, and a new
bank-partnership package providing accident, life, education and marriage benefits.
Audit transformation. Creation of a unified Chief Auditor office and rollout of the

Comprehensive Audit Management System (CAMS) and Audit Para Management
System (APMS).
Catalytic infrastructure finance. Through TNIFMC, the State has mobilised ≈₹1,363
crore across a Shelter Fund, an Emerging-Sector Seed Fund and a Green Climate Fund,
alongside a large PPP project pipeline under TNIDB and externally aided urban
programmes.
Heritage and climate as flagship agendas. Major archaeological investment (Keeladi,
Gangaikonda Cholapuram, Mayiladumparai’s Iron-Age dating) and a wide
climate/pollution-control programme (Meendum Manjappai, district climate missions,
measurable PM10 reductions).

2.2 GSDP Wealth Creation (independent analysis)
An independent analytical column (Mudar Patherya, Trisys Communications) appraised chief
ministers as “investment portfolio managers” using a restricted nominal-dollar GSDP
dataset (2011-12 to 2024-25). On that basis Tamil Nadu’s economy expanded from about
$241 billion (2020-21) to roughly $369 billion (2024-25) — estimated wealth creation of
≈$128 billion, averaging ≈$32 billion a year — which the author characterises as
outperformance relative to several peers. The column also singles out Tamil Nadu (with
Karnataka) for industrial depth, export sophistication and formal-sector strength.
This is a journalistic/analytical assessment, not official data. The author explicitly
cautions that the figures are nominal-dollar, indicative rather than precise, drawn from a
restricted dataset, and influenced by inherited momentum and external factors. It is
included here only as directional context.

3. Treasury & Accounts Department
3.1 Mandate and Scale
Among the oldest functional arms of government, reorganised as a separate department in
1962 under Finance. It operates through 6 zonal Joint-Director offices, 6 Pay & Accounts
Offices, 3 Sub-Pay & Accounts Offices, 38 District Treasuries, 1 Pension Pay Office, 1
Stamp-supervision office and 243 Sub-Treasuries.
Core functions include: handling all government revenue and expenditure; salary disbursal
to 9.2 lakh+ employees; pension to 7.4 lakh+ pensioners; releasing scheme funds;
monthly accounts to the State AG; stamp-paper sales; and administering the new medicalinsurance schemes.

3.2 IFHRMS 2.0 (“Kaniniyam”) — the Digital Backbone
A unified finance-plus-HR platform implemented statewide (first piloted in Karur, January
2020), now covering all district treasuries and pay-accounts offices. Key features:
Salary and all bill payments routed through IFHRMS and credited via RBI e-Kuber
directly to beneficiary bank accounts.
e-Challan / e-Receipt facility enabling 24×7 online payment of government dues; four
aggregator banks (SBI, Indian Bank, IOB, Canara) authorised, with revenue swept into

the State’s RBI account; integrated with Commercial Taxes and Prohibition & Excise.
RBI NEFT/RTGS live since 24.04.2023 for instant receipt of revenue.
Kaniniyam mobile app (launched 27.02.2024) for leave, advances, pension proposals,
CPS balances, deposits, income-tax regime selection, online life-certificate (Aadhaar
face authentication) and treasury transfer.
Modules: OPPAS (electronic pension/gratuity/GPF settlement), DAAS (digital
auditing/accounting; paperless e-Vouchers, AG exemption from 01.02.2025), automatic
income-tax computation, CPS final settlement, NPS contributions to NSDL, and
duplicate-beneficiary detection (CMUPT, OAP, SSN-IG) to prevent fraudulent/double
payments.
Welfare disbursals routed through the system include 14 lakh Old-Age Pension
beneficiaries, 8 lakh Muthulakshmi scheme beneficiaries, and Moovalur Ramamirtham
(girls’ education) transfers.
Ongoing integrations: Transport and Registration departments, TANGEDCO (piloted from
01.01.2025 in Erode; Ranipet, Theni, Mayiladuthurai from 01.02.2025; all districts from
01.04.2025), BSNL bill payments, income-tax e-filing (24Q/24G/26Q), and a Global
Beneficiary Master with large-batch (up to 10 lakh) disbursal capability.

3.3 Pensioners’ Family Security Fund
Scheme operative since 1997; current lump-sum ₹50,000 to the nominee/eligible family on
a pensioner’s death. To clear a COVID-era backlog (May 2021–Jan 2024), the State
advanced ₹121 crore from government account; 25,000 pending claims were settled and
credited directly, benefiting 25,000 families. (A sidebar in the source cites ₹125 crore — see
Section 14.)

3.4 New Medical Insurance Schemes (2021 & 2022)
Implemented through United India Insurance Co. Ltd.
Employees (New Medical Insurance Scheme 2021):
Cover of ₹5 lakh (2021–2025); monthly deduction ₹300 (₹295 premium + ₹5 corpus).
Extended to 30.06.2026.
Special diseases ceiling raised to ₹10 lakh, with a further ₹10 lakh from corpus — total
up to ₹20 lakh.
203 treatments/surgical procedures; 1,497 hospitals + 16 government radiationtherapy centres; non-network emergency treatment reimbursed at 75%; dependent
sons/daughters covered without age limit (from 30.12.2021); COVID-19 added

(14.02.2022).
Outcome: 3,33,675 beneficiaries, ₹1,706.19 crore in treatment availed.
Pensioners (Scheme 2022, 01.07.2022–30.10.2025):
Comparable structure; 1,490 hospitals networked.
Outcome: 3,37,338 beneficiaries, ₹1,447.91 crore.
A dedicated medical monitoring team (8 posts) created to oversee insurer
performance; COVID corpus funds of ₹87.10 crore (network) and ₹17.47 crore (nonnetwork) disbursed.

3.5 Pension-Process Simplification
Year-round interview facility for pensioners (since 01.07.2023) — life certificate
submitted in the month of retirement/commencement, reducing hardship.
Automatic conversion of service pension to family pension on the pensioner’s death,
with spouse details pre-loaded in IFHRMS — no fresh paperwork required.

3.6 State Government Employee Account (Bank MoU, 19.05.2025)
MoU with seven banks (SBI, IOB, Indian Bank, Canara, Axis, Bank of Baroda, Union Bank)
for free value-added benefits on salary accounts:
Personal-accident cover of ₹1 crore (death/permanent disability);
Up to ₹10 lakh for higher education and ₹5 lakh–₹10 lakh for children’s marriage of
deceased employees;
₹10 lakh life cover for natural death in service; concessional loan rates.
To date: ₹20.65 crore paid to heirs of 46 deceased employees (18 accidental — ₹15.80
crore; 28 natural — ₹5.28 crore).

3.7 Buildings & Codification
New own-buildings for several District and Sub-Treasuries inaugurated (notably on
25.10.2021 and 26.08.2022); fresh sub-treasury buildings under construction (e.g.,
Arani ₹1.26 crore, Thottiyam ₹1.19 crore). (Several building cost figures in the source

The integrated Finance complex named “Professor K. Anbazhagan Maaligai”
(19.12.2021).
Rewriting of the TN Treasury Code, manuals and the TN Financial Code Vols. I & II

entrusted to ICAI-ARF (financial sanction ₹57.82 lakh); draft/initial reports under
government approval.
TN Public Finance Management System (TNPFMS): an end-to-end
revenue/expenditure-monitoring platform; 99 schemes across 40 departments
onboarded, with last-mile beneficiary tracking.

3.8 Recruitment & Capacity Building
Direct-recruit Accounts Officers filled in tranches via TNPSC (16 + 5 + 11 selections; SC
carry-forward vacancies honoured).
Refresher/need-based training delivered with ICAI and IIT-Madras; allocations of ₹30
lakh (2023-24), ₹50 lakh (current year), ₹16.19 lakh + ₹70.72 lakh (senior officers), and
₹1.19 crore (new recruits/IFHRMS training); 213 senior officers and 645 direct recruits
trained, among others.

4. Audit Reforms
4.1 Unified Chief Auditor
To bring all audit wings under Finance and standardise internal and statutory audit, a Chief
Auditor (Thalaimai Thanikkai Iyakkunar) office was created — covering Local Fund, Cooperative, Milk Co-operative, State Government and HR&CE audits.

4.2 Digital Audit Platforms
CAMS (Comprehensive Audit Management System): live from 15.04.2025, digitising
audit from initiation to report and follow-up; Q1–Q2 of 2025-26 completed on CAMS, Q3
under way — improving transparency for audited entities.
APMS (Audit Para Management System): live from 17.11.2025 to track resolution of
audit objections.
IDEA software for data-analytics audit; 100% e-Office in the State Government Audit
wing.

4.3 Objection Resolution & Performance Audit
Objections cleared: 81,354 non-monetary paras and 2,049 monetary paras
(₹2,883.22 lakh); high-level committees at district/department/board levels.
Performance audit introduced (2023) for charitable institutions — Annadhanam,

temple-tank upkeep, asset and crowd/sanitation management — with Template Audit
for ~18,000 small institutions.
Audit fees of ₹528.41 crore collected over four years; a Code of Ethics issued to all
auditors; reports now approved “one step below” (zonal heads) to cut delays.

4.4 Recruitment & Milk Co-operative Audit
918 Junior Co-operative Auditors + 8 Assistant Directors recruited via TNPSC
(2024).
Milk co-operatives: 53,280 primary societies audited (a record); Aavin federation
and district unions audited largely up to date (2020-21 to 2024-25 progressively
certified).

5. Small Savings Department
Collections rose sharply since 07.05.2021, led by Post Office Savings, Monthly Income
Scheme, Recurring Deposit and Senior Citizens’ Savings Scheme.
Agent incentives: 3,141 beneficiaries, ₹41.60 lakh (MPKBY/SAS agents).
World Thrift Day (30 Oct): state/district essay, poetry and speech contests — 4,851
beneficiaries, ₹18.10 lakh.
Awareness street plays: 1,250 plays, ₹10 lakh; government exhibitions, ₹7.19 lakh;
further media/serial-drama campaigns planned.

10. Cross-Cutting Themes
1. Digital-first administration — IFHRMS 2.0, CAMS/APMS, TNPFMS, e-Challan/e-Receipt
and the Kaniniyam app collectively shift the portfolio toward real-time, paperless,
beneficiary-direct operations.

2. Fiscal self-reliance — revenue-department integration and faster receipt mechanisms
reinforce TN’s already-high own-revenue share (Section 2.1).
3. Workforce social security — medical insurance, family security fund, and the bankMoU package form a layered safety net for employees and pensioners.
4. Transparency & accountability — unified audit, ethics code, decentralised report
approval and digital objection-tracking.
5. Climate-aligned growth — green funds, district climate missions and pollution
monitoring tie infrastructure financing to the 2070 net-zero goal.
6. Heritage as soft power & local economy — Keeladi/Gangaikonda Cholapuram
investments combine scientific credibility with tourism-led local livelihoods.

12. Observations & Recommendations for Policy Makers
1. Lock in the own-revenue gains. The recovery to ~75% own-revenue share is
impressive but partly cyclical. Completing revenue-department integrations (Transport,
Registration, TANGEDCO, GST data) into IFHRMS, and publishing a quarterly receipts
dashboard, would make the gain structural and auditable.
2. Pre-fund the social-security schemes. The Family Security Fund needed a ₹121 crore
bailout because subscriptions did not cover claims. An actuarial review and a defined
corpus-replenishment rule would prevent recurrence; the same discipline should be
applied to the medical-insurance corpus.
3. Track CAMS/APMS outcomes, not just rollout. Now that the platforms are live, publish
objection-closure rates, recovery amounts and time-to-resolution by department to
convert digitisation into demonstrable accountability.
4. Strengthen the PPP pipeline’s bankability. The TNIDB list is broad; policy makers
should prioritise by financial close achieved and risk allocation, and report the realised
PPP investment (not just approvals) to maintain investor confidence.
5. Mainstream the climate funds. TNGCF and TNESSF are still early-stage relative to
TNSF. Clear deal pipelines, co-investment terms and measured carbon outcomes would
help scale them toward the 2070 net-zero target.
6. Resolve data-quality gaps before external publication (see Section 14) — a single
source of truth for headline figures would strengthen credibility with legislators, lenders
and rating agencies.

13. Data Caveats & Methodological Notes
Out-of-order source. The departmental text was a jumbled extraction; sections have
been re-sequenced by department. Some passages were duplicated across pages
(employee vs. pensioner medical-insurance text in particular) and have been deduplicated.
Internal inconsistencies in the source:
Family Security Fund advance cited as ₹125 crore (sidebar) and ₹121 crore (body)
— the body figure is used.
Several treasury-building costs appear transcribed in “lakh” where “crore” is
almost certainly intended (e.g., a district treasury at “₹3.12 lakh”); only clearly-

stated crore figures have been reproduced, and dubious values are omitted.
Medical-insurance coverage/ceiling values appear in the source as “₹5,000,000 /
₹10,000,000” and are read here as ₹5 lakh / ₹10 lakh / ₹20 lakh per standard
scheme parameters.
The TNIDB “2030 economy target” figure in the extraction is corrupted (“5 million
dollar”); it has been described qualitatively rather than quoted, as it does not match
the State’s known trillion-dollar-economy aspiration.
Hospital counts differ slightly between the employee scheme (1,497) and pensioner
scheme (1,490) sections; both are reproduced as stated.
GSDP figures (Section 2.2) are from an independent opinion column using nominaldollar, restricted-dataset methodology with the author’s own disclaimers; treat as
directional, not official.
Table A.2 values are RBI estimates; 2024-25 are Revised Estimates and 2025-26 are
Budget Estimates.
